Understanding what healthy looks like

Knowledge is the first step. These short reads help you identify patterns, name feelings, and start making sense of your experiences.

⭐ Start here

5 signs a relationship might not be as healthy as it feels

Sometimes things feel fine because we have adapted to them. These signs are not accusations — they are gentle nudges to pay attention.

1
You often feel confused after a conversation that started simply.
2
You change your behaviour to avoid a bad reaction from them.
3
Apologies come, but the same things keep happening.
4
You feel worse about yourself than before this relationship.
5
Your feelings are regularly dismissed or minimised.
🔄
Patterns

What is the cycle of tension and repair?

Many harmful dynamics have a recognisable rhythm — tension builds, something happens, there is a honeymoon period, and it starts again.

The repair phase can feel so good — loving, full of remorse — that it resets your sense of reality. Recognising the cycle is not about blame; it is about seeing the full picture.
🧠
Emotional

Why it is hard to trust your own memory

When someone consistently questions your recollection of events, it can slowly make you doubt your own mind. This is not your fault.

This experience can leave you feeling foggy and reliant on the other person to tell you what happened. Keeping a private journal can help you reconnect with your own account of things.
🤝
What healthy looks like

Disagreements in a healthy relationship

Conflict is normal — all couples and families argue. The difference is in how it happens and how it ends.

In a healthy disagreement, both people feel heard even if they do not agree. Neither person uses silence as punishment or brings up unrelated past mistakes.
🌱
Growth

Understanding your own patterns

Sometimes the most useful thing is to look at the role we play — not with blame, but with curiosity.

People often develop protective patterns in childhood that no longer serve them in adult relationships — things like people-pleasing or emotional shutdown. Recognising these with compassion is the beginning of real change.

FAQ

Questions people are afraid to ask

No question is too small or too embarrassing. These are the ones we hear most often.

Is it normal to feel more anxious around someone you love?
Love can sometimes feel complicated, but ongoing anxiety around a specific person — especially about how they will react — is worth paying attention to. Feeling relaxed and safe with someone you love is not an unrealistic expectation.
What if they are kind most of the time?
The good times are real — and they make everything else more confusing. The question is not whether someone is kind sometimes, but whether the unkind moments are causing you real pain and whether the pattern is changing over time.
What if I am the one causing harm?
Asking this question already says something important about you. The fact that you are asking means you are doing something many people cannot. Speaking to a counsellor is one of the most courageous things you can do.
Why do I keep going back even when I know it is not right?
This is incredibly common and has nothing to do with weakness. Attachment, hope, fear, and neurological patterns can all make leaving feel impossible. Please do not judge yourself. Reaching out — like you are doing now — is already part of the process.
My friend will not listen to me. What do I do?
People often need to reach their own conclusions in their own time. The most powerful thing you can do is stay present and let them know you are there without judgment — no matter what they decide.
How do I talk to someone without making them defensive?
Lead with curiosity instead of conclusions. Instead of "I think your partner is controlling", try "I have noticed you seem stressed — how are you feeling?" Sharing observations about how your friend seems tends to open conversations rather than close them.
Is ThinkAgain a crisis service?
No — ThinkAgain is a reflection and awareness tool, not a clinical or crisis service. If you or someone you know is in immediate danger, please contact emergency services. The Resources section has links to trained helplines.
When should I speak to a professional?
Any time you feel ready — you do not need to be in crisis to benefit from talking to a counsellor. If you are feeling consistently anxious, confused, or unsafe in a relationship, these are all good reasons to reach out.
